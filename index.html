About me

I am a PhD student in Computer Science at Michigan State University with a strong background in machine learning and natural language processing. My research focuses on developing methods for extracting patterns from small, complex datasets, with applications in healthcare, language, and data analysis.

I have published at NAACL and COLM, and demonstrated the ability to lead interdisciplinary research projects and collaborate across domains. My work bridges the gap between theoretical advances in AI and practical applications that can benefit underserved communities.

Research Interests

  • AI in Healthcare

    Leveraging large language models to generalize patterns learned from scarce medical datasets, enabling better predictions and insights for underserved populations.

  • Long-Tail Generation

    Investigating and enhancing how large language models understand and generate rare linguistic phenomena, including idioms and figurative language.

  • AI in Education

    Developing intelligent systems that personalize classroom experiences by tailoring lessons to individual student needs and learning styles.

Teaching

Teaching Philosophy

My goal as an instructor is to help students develop practical and professional skills through collaborative, hands-on learning that mirrors real-world software development. I believe that students learn best by actively building rather than passively consuming information. I structure courses around core concepts followed by guided application where students apply ideas to real-world problems.

I view the classroom as an interactive workshop rather than a traditional lecture environment. Students work individually and in teams on incremental tasks that build toward larger software systems, experiencing the full software development lifecycle including requirement analysis, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance.

My assessment philosophy focuses on authentic demonstrations of understanding through design artifacts, code reviews, written reflections, and collaborative activities. These assessments measure not only technical correctness but also software quality, maintainability, and communication. Regular feedback opportunities allow students to revise and improve their work throughout the semester, emphasizing that software engineering is an iterative discipline centered on continuous improvement.
